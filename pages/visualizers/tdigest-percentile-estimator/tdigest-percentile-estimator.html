t-Digest Percentile Estimator

p99 latency for a billion requests, from a few hundred numbers.

t-Digest maintains a small set of weighted "centroids" instead of storing every value — and deliberately keeps them SMALL near the tails (p99, p99.9) and LARGE near the median. This non-uniform sizing is the entire trick: it spends its limited precision budget exactly where extreme-percentile accuracy actually matters.

📈 Why Uneven Centroid Sizing Works

⚖️

The Scale Function

A centroid spanning quantile range [q_low, q_high] is allowed a maximum size proportional to q(1−q). Near q=0.5, this is at its largest (0.25) — near q=0 or q=1, it shrinks toward zero. This single function is what makes tail centroids small automatically.

🔗

Sequential Merging

New values merge into the current centroid if doing so wouldn't exceed its size limit for its current quantile position; otherwise a new centroid starts. This single pass keeps the digest compact and self-adjusting to any distribution shape.

🎯

Why p99 Matters More Than p50

A slow p50 means half your users have a mediocre experience. A slow p99 means your worst 1% — often your angriest, most vocal users — have a terrible one. Monitoring systems obsess over tail percentiles precisely because that's where user pain concentrates.

🏢

Production Scale

Datadog and Prometheus's histogram-based summaries use t-digest or similar sketches to report accurate p99/p99.9 latency across millions of requests per second, using a fixed memory budget of a few hundred centroids per metric — regardless of total request volume.
